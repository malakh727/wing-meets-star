The Earth is surrounded by an atmosphere consisting of several gases, the most important of which are oxygen and nitrogen. When sunlight reaches the globe and interacts with this atmosphere, it produces a blue sky.

White light consists of several colors, each with a specific wavelength. Red light has a large wavelength, while blue has a shorter one.

When sunlight enters the atmosphere, the rays collide with the gas particles that make up the atmosphere. Long-wavelength colors — such as red and yellow — pass directly through them, forming the sunlight we see from Earth's surface. Short-wavelength colors, like blue, are reflected and scattered, forming the distinctive color of the sky.

The sky tends toward red at sunrise and sunset because the sun is ostensibly farther from Earth during those times. Red light, with its large wavelength, is the first to scatter — dyeing the clouds with stunning colors of red, orange, and pink.
