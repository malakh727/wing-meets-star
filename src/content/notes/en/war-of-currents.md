The war of the currents — a war of another kind, a scientific war.

This term goes back to the nineteenth century. The rivalry was between George Westinghouse and Nikola Tesla and their alternating current on the one hand, and Thomas Edison and his direct current on the other — a battle over which electricity distribution system would power factories and light homes.

In the beginning, Edison's direct current was the system approved by the United States. But it suffered many problems: it could not transmit current over long distances and required many generators. The idea of alternating current solved these problems while also saving costs. Edison, however, doubted these improvements and continued marketing his own system while criticizing the other side.

In 1893, the two teams competed to light the Chicago World's Fair. Westinghouse prevailed when he invented an electric lamp powered by alternating current at lower economic costs.

Westinghouse and Tesla won. Alternating current was adopted and spread around the world in the fields of electrical power generation, transmission, and distribution.
