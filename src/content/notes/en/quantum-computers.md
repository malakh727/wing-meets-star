A bit is the smallest measurement unit on a regular computer. It may be 0 or 1, with or without current.

But in a quantum computer it is different. The quantum bit, or "qubit", can represent both states at the same time — zero and one together — depending on the principles of quantum mechanics and its phenomena. Therefore, two units of qubits produce 4 possible states, and three qubits produce 8 possible states. As the number of units grows, so do the possible states, requiring enormous storage space.

Due to the instability of the qubit unit, it needs very low temperatures to sustain.

As quantum processors become smaller and more stable, we will be able to see their use in a decade or less.

Interest in the quantum computer came from the remarkable results expected from its implementation: we will need less time, effort and energy to perform complex calculations that may take years, and we will succeed in developing better artificial intelligence and machine learning.
