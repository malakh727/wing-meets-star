We celebrate shipping fast. We celebrate moving before we are ready. We celebrate the minimum viable product, the rough draft, the prototype-as-product. There is wisdom in this, but also a cost we rarely name.

Some things require slowness to exist at all.

## What speed costs

When you build fast, you build with what you already know. Speed is the enemy of the unfamiliar. It keeps you on well-worn paths, using familiar tools, solving problems the way you have solved them before.

The interesting problems — the ones worth solving — almost always require you to learn something you do not know yet. And learning takes time. It cannot be rushed without becoming something else: mimicry, approximation, the appearance of understanding.

## Deliberateness is a skill

Building slowly is not about being careful in the sense of timid. It is about being *deliberate* — about knowing why each decision was made, about being able to trace the line between the problem and the solution.

Deliberate builders ask: *What exactly am I solving? What am I not solving? What assumptions am I making? What would I have to believe for this to be wrong?*

These questions take time. They are not compatible with sprinting.

## The long arc

I have noticed that the things I built slowly are the things I still understand. The fast ones have become mysterious to me — artifacts of a past version of myself who was in a hurry for reasons I no longer remember.

Slowness is a form of respect. Respect for the problem. Respect for the people who will encounter the solution. Respect for future-you, who will have to live with what present-you decided.

Build slowly. Build like it matters. It probably does.
