There is a question I return to often: what separates the engineers who build things that last from those who merely build things that work?

It is not experience. It is not tools. It is the quality of their questions.

## Curiosity as a method

Most people treat curiosity as something that happens to them — a spark, a mood, an accident. But the engineers I admire most treat it as a *discipline*. They ask questions on a schedule. They keep a running list of things they do not understand. They follow threads even when they seem to lead nowhere.

This is the engineering of curiosity: the deliberate construction of a questioning mind.

## The practice

A few things I have found useful:

**Keep a confusion log.** When something does not make sense, write it down. Do not resolve it immediately. Let it sit. Often, the act of articulating what confuses you is half the answer.

**Ask the question one level deeper.** When you find an answer, pause. Then ask why *that* is true. Most engineers stop at the first satisfying explanation. The interesting territory is always one layer further.

**Follow the anomaly.** When something works in a way you did not expect, that is not a lucky accident — it is an invitation. The gap between your model and reality is where learning lives.

## Why it matters

A curious engineer does not just solve the problem in front of them. They build a richer model of the world that makes the *next* problem easier to see.

Curiosity compounds. Ask enough questions over enough years, and you start to see connections that others miss — not because you are smarter, but because you have been paying closer attention for longer.

The wing meets the star not by force, but by finding the right angle of approach.
