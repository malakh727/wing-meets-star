She knew the sky the way a programmer knows a codebase — not by memorizing it, but by understanding its logic.

Every star was a function. Every constellation was a module. The relationships between them were the architecture: invisible, structural, load-bearing.

She had learned this from her grandmother, who learned it from hers, in a line of women who crossed water without instruments and arrived exactly where they meant to. The knowledge passed down not in written form but in practice — in the repeated act of looking, calculating, correcting.

---

"How do you know?" the student asked one evening, as the ship moved through water that looked identical in every direction.

"I watch what changes," she said. "The stars don't move. But the angle changes. The timing changes. I watch what changes and deduce what's constant."

---

This is the navigator's code: not a set of rules but a habit of attention. The assumption that there is an underlying structure. The patience to let it reveal itself.

The stars are always broadcasting. The question is whether you have learned the frequency.
