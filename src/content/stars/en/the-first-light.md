Before there were names for stars, there was only the act of looking up.

A figure at the edge of a desert — no tools, no maps, no inherited knowledge of the sky. Just two eyes adapted to darkness and a sky so dense with light it seemed almost solid.

She did not know what she was looking at. She only knew it was *important*.

This is the first act of science: not hypothesis, not method, not experiment — but the recognition that something matters. The feeling that *this* deserves attention, that *this* is worth sitting with in the dark until it begins to speak.

---

Centuries later, we have names for everything she saw. We have coordinates and magnitudes and spectral classifications. We have mapped the distances in light-years, which is to say: in time, which is to say: in the deep impossibility of scale.

And still, sometimes, the right thing to do is to put the tools down and just look.

The star does not need your understanding to burn.

But you — you might need its light.
